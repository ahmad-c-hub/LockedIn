🟣 Memory Domain

Memory Matrix

Memory Working Memory Visual-Spatial

What It Does

A grid illuminates a pattern of cells in sequence. The player must observe the pattern, then recreate it precisely from memory. Grid size scales from 3×3 to 4×4 and sequences grow longer with each level keeping the brain's working memory load in the adaptive challenge zone.

Cognitive Target

Visuospatial working memory the mental scratchpad that holds and manipulates visual and spatial information. This is one of the most trainable cognitive systems and directly underlies skills from navigation to reading comprehension.

Research Foundation

Baddeley & Hitch (1974) established the multi-component working memory model, identifying the visuospatial sketchpad as a distinct and trainable system. Klingberg et al. (2002) showed that working memory training in children with ADHD produced lasting improvements visible on fMRI increased activity in prefrontal and parietal circuits. Jaeggi et al. (2008) extended this to show working memory training transfers to fluid intelligence gains.

Baddeley, A.D. & Hitch, G. (1974). Working Memory. Psychology of Learning and Motivation, 8, 47–89. | Klingberg, T. et al. (2002). Training of working memory in children with ADHD. J. Clin. Exp. Neuropsychol., 24(6), 781–791.

Expected Outcomes

  • Increased working memory capacity (Miller's 7±2 chunks expanding toward higher limits)
  • Improved academic performance, particularly in math and reading
  • Reduced mind-wandering during focused tasks
  • Better performance on novel problem-solving tasks (fluid intelligence)

Why We Integrated It

Working memory is the single most important trainable cognitive resource. It predicts academic success better than IQ in some studies (Alloway & Alloway, 2010) and is the primary bottleneck for focus when working memory is overloaded, attention fails. By making Memory Matrix the foundational game in LockedIn, we directly target the root cause of focus deficits rather than their symptoms.

🔵

Pattern Trail

Memory Sequence Memory Spatial Memory

What It Does

Dots appear one-by-one on screen in a specific spatial sequence. After all dots have been shown, the player must tap them back in the exact order of appearance. The grid grows (3×3 → 4×4) and sequence length increases from 3 to 12 dots across 10 levels.

Cognitive Target

Sequential and spatial working memory the ability to maintain an ordered series of locations in mind. This is distinct from basic memory and maps to the brain's hippocampal-parietal spatial navigation network.

Research Foundation

Nissen & Bullemer (1987) demonstrated that humans implicitly learn spatial sequences through repeated exposure, activating the basal ganglia and hippocampus. Burgess (2002) showed the hippocampal–parietal network is critical for spatial sequence memory. The Corsi block-tapping test on which this game is directly modeled has been used in neuropsychological assessment since 1972 and is a validated measure of visuospatial working memory span.

Nissen, M.J. & Bullemer, P. (1987). Attentional requirements of learning. Cognitive Psychology, 19(1), 1–32. | Corsi, P.M. (1972). Human memory and the medial temporal region of the brain. Dissertation, McGill University.

Expected Outcomes

  • Expanded visuospatial memory span
  • Improved sequential planning ability
  • Strengthened hippocampal-parietal connections supporting navigation and spatial reasoning
  • Better recall of procedural sequences in real-world tasks

Why We Integrated It

Pattern Trail complements Memory Matrix by targeting a distinct but equally critical memory system sequential spatial memory. The two games together train the full scope of the visuospatial sketchpad and build the foundation for complex planning tasks that rely on holding multiple ordered steps in mind.

Visual N-Back

Memory Working Memory Daily Challenge

What It Does

A 3×3 grid lights up one cell at a time in a continuous stream. The player must judge, for each new stimulus, whether it matches the cell that lit up exactly 2 steps earlier (2-back task). 30 trials per session with 2-second response windows demanding sustained attention and rapid working memory updating.

Cognitive Target

Working memory updating the executive process of refreshing and replacing information held in mind. This is the most cognitively demanding and highest-value training task in LockedIn, directly linked to fluid intelligence.

Research Foundation

Jaeggi et al. (2008) published a landmark paper in PNAS demonstrating that dual n-back training produced significant, transfer gains in fluid intelligence the first time any training was shown to improve this previously believed immutable trait. Sussman (2012) replicated improvements in working memory capacity. The prefrontal cortex's dorsolateral region central to fluid reasoning is the primary neural site activated during n-back performance.

Jaeggi, S.M. et al. (2008). Improving fluid intelligence with training on working memory. PNAS, 105(19), 6829–6833. | Sussman, R.J. (2012). N-back training task performance. Applied Cognitive Psychology, 26(5).

Expected Outcomes

  • Measurable gains in fluid intelligence (Gf)
  • Increased working memory capacity and updating speed
  • Improved academic and professional performance requiring novel reasoning
  • Reduction in ADHD-like attention failures

Why We Integrated It

The n-back task is arguably the most well-studied cognitive training paradigm in neuroscience. Its daily inclusion in LockedIn as a special challenge task reflects its singular importance it targets fluid intelligence at its source and represents the gold-standard benchmark for working memory training.

🎨

Color Match (Stroop Task)

Attention Inhibitory Control Cognitive Flexibility

What It Does

A word (e.g., "RED") appears printed in a conflicting ink color (e.g., blue). The player must tap the button matching the ink color, suppressing the automatic impulse to respond to the word meaning. Levels 1–3 match color, 4–7 match shape, 8–10 require matching both simultaneously.

Cognitive Target

Inhibitory control the prefrontal cortex's executive mechanism for suppressing automatic, prepotent responses in favor of goal-directed behavior. This is the foundational skill that underpins self-regulation, focus, and resistance to distraction.

Research Foundation

The Stroop task (Stroop, 1935) is one of the most replicated paradigms in cognitive psychology MacLeod's (1991) review cited over 700 studies in 50 years. The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) activate during conflict resolution. Diamond (2013) identified inhibitory control as the most critical executive function, underpinning attention, self-regulation, and academic success. Training the Stroop repeatedly strengthens these inhibitory circuits.

Stroop, J.R. (1935). Studies of interference in serial verbal reactions. J. Experimental Psychology, 18(6), 643. | MacLeod, C.M. (1991). Half a century of research on the Stroop effect. Psychological Bulletin, 109(2), 163.

Expected Outcomes

  • Strengthened inhibitory control, reducing impulsive reactions
  • Greater cognitive flexibility in switching between mental rules
  • Reduced susceptibility to environmental distractions
  • Improved performance on all tasks requiring top-down attentional control

Why We Integrated It

The Stroop task directly trains the same neural circuit that gets worn down by excessive social media use the prefrontal inhibitory control system. Every time a user scrolls against their intention, that system has failed. Color Match builds it back up. It is the most direct cognitive antidote to the attention-hijacking design of modern apps.

Train of Thought

Attention Multi-Object Tracking Working Memory

What It Does

Colored trains rush toward matching colored stations on a branching track network. The player taps junction switches to route each train correctly before it crashes. Multiple trains run simultaneously, requiring continuous divided attention and real-time spatial decision-making.

Cognitive Target

Multiple object tracking (MOT) the ability to simultaneously monitor several moving objects. This demands sustained attention, divided attention allocation, and rapid decision-making under time pressure a complex attentional skill with broad real-world relevance.

Research Foundation

Pylyshyn & Storm (1988) demonstrated that the brain can track 4–5 independent objects using a parallel tracking mechanism, distinct from serial attention. Cavanagh & Alvarez (2005) showed MOT training improves attentional capacity. The task activates bilateral parietal cortex the spatial attention network and the frontal eye fields responsible for attentional guidance. Improved MOT performance predicts better real-world multi-tasking and driving performance.

Pylyshyn, Z.W. & Storm, R.W. (1988). Tracking multiple independent targets. Spatial Vision, 3(3), 179–197. | Cavanagh, P. & Alvarez, G.A. (2005). Tracking multiple targets with multifocal attention. Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 9(7), 349–354.

Expected Outcomes

  • Expanded attentional capacity tracking more objects without loss
  • Faster attentional switching between simultaneous tasks
  • Improved performance in complex real-world environments
  • Strengthened parietal spatial attention networks

Why We Integrated It

Focused attention on a single object is only one form of attention. Many high-demand cognitive tasks driving, managing multiple projects, listening in noisy environments require simultaneous multi-object attention. Train of Thought builds this capacity in a uniquely engaging format that no other standard cognitive training task provides.

Go / No-Go

Attention Response Inhibition Impulse Control

What It Does

Green circles (Go) and red circles (No-Go) appear at random. The player must tap the green and withhold response to the red sounds trivial until the pace accelerates to the point where inhibiting the prepotent tap response requires genuine effortful control.

Cognitive Target

Response inhibition the core executive function of stopping an already-initiated action. Critically impaired in ADHD, substance use disorders, and obsessive-compulsive tendencies. The right inferior frontal gyrus and subthalamic nucleus are the primary neural substrates.

Research Foundation

Aron & Poldrack (2005) mapped the neural circuitry of response inhibition using fMRI during Go/No-Go tasks, identifying the right inferior frontal cortex (rIFC) as the "stop signal" region. Repeated inhibition training strengthens the hyperdirect pathway from rIFC to subthalamic nucleus. Nigg (2001) showed inhibitory control is the central neurocognitive deficit in ADHD. Go/No-Go training is a standard component of cognitive training programs for ADHD and impulse-control disorders.

Aron, A.R. & Poldrack, R.A. (2005). The cognitive neuroscience of response inhibition. Biological Psychiatry, 57(11), 1285–1292. | Nigg, J.T. (2001). Is ADHD a disinhibitory disorder? Psychological Bulletin, 127(5), 571–598.

Expected Outcomes

  • Reduced commission errors (acting when you shouldn't)
  • Faster and more reliable response inhibition in daily life
  • Improved impulse control, particularly under cognitive load
  • Strengthened right inferior frontal cortex the brain's "stop" system

Why We Integrated It

The inability to stop checking one's phone even when consciously trying not to is a failure of response inhibition. Go/No-Go directly trains the neural circuit responsible. By including it as a daily challenge, LockedIn builds the biological capacity to resist digital compulsions that undermine focus.

Flanker Task

Attention Selective Attention Conflict Resolution

What It Does

Five arrows appear in a row. The player must respond to the direction of the center arrow only, ignoring the surrounding four "flanker" arrows that either point the same way (congruent) or the opposite direction (incongruent). Incongruent trials create attentional conflict that the anterior cingulate cortex must resolve.

Cognitive Target

Selective attention and conflict monitoring the ability to extract a relevant signal from surrounding noise. This is the core of what people mean when they say they "can't focus in a loud environment" or "get easily distracted."

Research Foundation

Eriksen & Eriksen (1974) introduced the flanker paradigm, demonstrating that irrelevant surrounding stimuli interfere with target processing a direct laboratory model of real-world distraction. Fan et al. (2002) used the attention network test (ANT which combines flankers with other cues) to map three distinct attentional networks: alerting (norepinephrine), orienting (parietal), and executive control (anterior cingulate). Flanker training specifically strengthens the executive control network.

Eriksen, B.A. & Eriksen, C.W. (1974). Effects of noise letters upon the identification of a target letter. Perception & Psychophysics, 16(1), 143–149. | Fan, J. et al. (2002). Testing the efficiency and independence of attentional networks. J. Cognitive Neuroscience, 14(3), 340–347.

Expected Outcomes

  • Faster conflict resolution getting less slowed down by irrelevant stimuli
  • Reduced flanker interference effect (measurable via reaction time)
  • Strengthened anterior cingulate cortex executive control network
  • Improved ability to focus in distracting, noisy environments

Why We Integrated It

The flanker task is the laboratory equivalent of trying to focus while notifications buzz, conversations happen nearby, and social media posts compete for attention. Every incongruent trial trains the brain to select the relevant signal and suppress interference exactly the skill modern life is eroding.

Speed Match

Speed Processing Speed Decision-Making

What It Does

Two cards appear sequentially. The player must instantly judge whether the current card matches the previous one and tap Yes or No before a countdown expires. Cards feature colors and shapes, and the response window shortens with each level forcing the brain to operate at increasing perceptual speed.

Cognitive Target

Processing speed the rate at which the brain can perceive, interpret, and respond to information. One of the strongest predictors of intelligence test performance, academic success, and cognitive aging trajectories.

Research Foundation

Salthouse (1996) proposed the processing-speed theory of cognitive aging demonstrating that decline in mental speed is the primary driver of age-related cognitive deterioration. Jensen (2006) showed processing speed (measured by simple and choice reaction times) correlates strongly with IQ. Edwards et al. (2002) demonstrated that speed-of-processing training in older adults improved everyday functional performance. The game's match/mismatch design specifically exercises the comparison process central to rapid perception.

Salthouse, T.A. (1996). The processing-speed theory of adult age differences in cognition. Psychological Review, 103(3), 403. | Jensen, A.R. (2006). Clocking the Mind: Mental Chronometry and Individual Differences. Elsevier.

Expected Outcomes

  • Faster perceptual discrimination and comparison
  • Reduced choice reaction time
  • Improved performance on time-pressured cognitive tasks
  • Better sustained performance under deadline pressure

Why We Integrated It

Cognitive slowness the feeling of your brain "lagging" is a key symptom of poor focus. By repeatedly training the brain to make rapid accurate judgments under time pressure, Speed Match directly improves the mental sharpness that most people associate with being "on" versus "foggy." It is also one of the most enjoyable and immediately rewarding training formats, critical for sustaining long-term engagement.

Number Stream

Speed Mental Arithmetic Working Memory

What It Does

Math equations (addition, subtraction, multiplication) scroll down the screen. The player must solve each one and tap the correct answer from four choices before the equation falls off the bottom. Speed and accuracy both score; equations grow in complexity and fall faster across levels.

Cognitive Target

Mental arithmetic under time pressure a compound task demanding working memory (holding intermediate values), processing speed (generating answers quickly), and selective attention (filtering answer choices). This is the rare game that trains three cognitive systems simultaneously.

Research Foundation

Ashcraft & Faust (1994) showed that math anxiety impairs working memory during arithmetic by occupying the central executive's attentional resources. DeStefano & LeFevre (2004) demonstrated that complex arithmetic relies heavily on phonological and visuospatial working memory buffers. Time-pressured arithmetic training is used in dyscalculia interventions (Wilson et al., 2006) and consistently improves working memory and processing speed in parallel.

Ashcraft, M.H. & Faust, M.W. (1994). Mathematics anxiety and mental arithmetic performance. Cognition & Emotion, 8(2), 97–125. | DeStefano, D. & LeFevre, J.A. (2004). The role of working memory in mental arithmetic. European J. Cognitive Psychology, 16(3), 353–386.

Expected Outcomes

  • Faster mental arithmetic and numerical fluency
  • Increased working memory capacity under dual-task conditions
  • Improved time-pressure performance across cognitive domains
  • Reduced math anxiety through desensitization and mastery

Why We Integrated It

Number Stream is LockedIn's highest-cognitive-load speed task, demanding simultaneous engagement of working memory, processing speed, and attention in every trial. This makes it uniquely effective for inducing neuroplastic change quickly the brain must form new shortcuts and automatize processes to keep up with increasing speed, producing rapid measurable gains in cognitive efficiency.

🔵 Logic & Executive Function Domain

🔷

Sudoku

Logic Executive Function Planning

What It Does

A 9×9 constraint-satisfaction puzzle where each row, column, and 3×3 box must contain digits 1–9 without repetition. Three difficulty modes (Easy, Medium, Hard) with timed completion, mistake tracking, and optional hints. The player must simultaneously apply logical deduction, hypothesis testing, and constraint propagation.

Cognitive Target

Executive function specifically planning, cognitive flexibility, and constraint satisfaction reasoning. These are the highest-order cognitive skills, residing in the prefrontal cortex and underlying all complex goal-directed behavior.

Research Foundation

Diamond (2013) in her comprehensive Annual Review paper identified planning, cognitive flexibility, and inhibition as the three core executive functions, all mediated by the prefrontal cortex. Stuss & Benson (1986) established that frontal lobe integrity is necessary for logical constraint application. Research on puzzle-solving (specifically number logic puzzles) by Verghese et al. (2003) in the New England Journal of Medicine showed that regular cognitively stimulating leisure activities reduce dementia risk by 47% with logic puzzles showing the strongest association.

Diamond, A. (2013). Executive functions. Annual Review of Psychology, 64, 135–168. | Verghese, J. et al. (2003). Leisure activities and the risk of dementia in the elderly. NEJM, 348(25), 2508–2516.

Expected Outcomes

  • Improved logical deduction and constraint reasoning
  • Greater cognitive flexibility in problem-solving approaches
  • Stronger prefrontal planning circuits
  • Reduced risk of cognitive decline with regular long-term engagement

Why We Integrated It

While most LockedIn games are short, intense, reaction-based training stimuli, Sudoku provides the sustained, slow, deep executive engagement that completes the cognitive training spectrum. Real-world cognitive performance requires not just fast reflexes and good memory, but also the capacity for sustained deliberate reasoning and Sudoku is the premier training vehicle for exactly that.
